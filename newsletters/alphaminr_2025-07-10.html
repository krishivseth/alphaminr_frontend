Digging Deeper Than the Headlines.

July 10, 2025

What happens when a global trade war collides with a softening labor market? That's the question rattling markets today as President Trump dramatically escalated trade tensions by threatening Brazil with a crippling 50% tariff starting August 1st, citing the ongoing prosecution of former President Bolsonaro and demanding an immediate end to what he called a "Witch Hunt" . The move sent shockwaves through commodity markets, as traders warned that US consumers will soon face sharp price increases on food staples like coffee, with coffee futures already jumping on the news .

The trade tensions come amid fresh signs of labor market weakness. Today's jobless claims data exceeded economists' expectations, with the US labor market showing significant cooling over the past year - a pullback attributed primarily to employers reining in hiring rather than conducting mass layoffs . Continuing claims are now running near four-year highs, indicating that people are having a harder time finding work . The timing couldn't be more precarious as economists warn that the early impacts of tariffs are "just one drag on the job market" amid "a bevy of other headwinds" including federal layoffs, slowing immigration, and high interest rates - with the combination threatening to decelerate the job market just as the full weight of tariff shocks arrives .

Adding to the uncertainty, Trump has promised at least seven more tariff letters to be delivered today, while also revealing plans for a 50% tariff on copper imports and threatening a massive 200% import tax on pharmaceuticals . He also announced plans to slap a 10% tariff on all BRICS nations, stating the economic bloc "was set up to hurt us" and "degenerate our dollar." The group now includes Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, as well as new members Egypt, Ethiopia, Indonesia, Iran and the UAE .

Beyond the Headlines

Brazil's coffee producers face an existential threat as Trump (GOP) slapped a 50% tariff on all Brazilian imports, explicitly citing the trial of former President Jair Bolsonaro for allegedly plotting a 2022 coup. "This Trial should not be taking place," Trump declared . While markets initially shrugged off earlier tariff threats, this one hits different - the US actually ran a $6.8 billion trade surplus with Brazil last year, with key US exports including aircraft, fuels, and industrial machinery. A retaliatory 50% Brazilian tariff could severely harm these American industries .

Wells Fargo (WFC) is sounding alarm bells on fresh recession risks after the bank announced it expects the Federal Reserve to keep rates steady at its July meeting . The concerns come as ADP's chief economist Nela Richardson noted that "though layoffs continue to be rare, a hesitancy to hire and a reluctance to replace departing workers led to job losses last month" . The bank's cautious stance reflects mounting evidence that the US labor market has cooled significantly during the past year, with the pullback in job growth largely attributed to employers reining in hiring rather than conducting mass layoffs .

The semiconductor sector faces new uncertainty as TSMC (TSM) navigates escalating trade tensions. While the company reported a 39% jump in Q2 sales driven by robust AI-driven chip demand , only the United Kingdom and Vietnam have reached framework agreements with the US so far, while China has merely agreed to a trade truce until August 12 . The lack of clarity on potential semiconductor restrictions has investors questioning the sustainability of TSMC's growth trajectory amid rising geopolitical risks.

Coffee futures surged today as Starbucks (SBUX) and other major coffee chains brace for supply chain disruptions. The commodity jumped sharply after Trump's Brazil tariff announcement, with industry analysts warning of an imminent price surge for US consumers . While the European Union has signaled willingness to accept a 10% universal tariff with sector exemptions and is racing to clinch a deal this week, Canada has made concessions by scrapping its digital services tax affecting US tech companies, with talks between the two countries targeting a mid-July agreement .

On the Horizon: Risks the Market is Missing

The market may be underestimating risks facing Microsoft (MSFT) as the AI boom enters a more complex phase. While investors remain focused on Azure's AI capabilities, growing trade tensions could disrupt the company's global cloud infrastructure buildout. The combination of semiconductor export controls, data sovereignty laws, and potential retaliation from BRICS nations threatens to fragment Microsoft's cloud strategy. If forced to maintain separate infrastructure stacks for different geopolitical blocs, Azure's economies of scale could deteriorate rapidly.

Analysts are overlooking a crucial vulnerability in McDonald's (MCD) supply chain as Brazil tariffs ripple through agricultural markets. The company sources significant volumes of beef, coffee, and other commodities from Brazil, with limited short-term alternatives for many ingredients. While McDonald's has some pricing power, the combination of 50% tariffs and potential retaliatory measures could compress margins by 200-300 basis points. The company's heavy reliance on standardized global menu items makes rapid supplier diversification particularly challenging.

The consensus bull case for Nvidia (NVDA) fails to account for mounting risks to its AI chip monopoly. While the company dominates the training market with its H100 GPUs, export controls and industrial policy shifts could accelerate development of domestic alternatives in China and other BRICS nations. The combination of technology transfer restrictions, semiconductor manufacturing constraints, and potential emergence of rival chip architectures optimized for different AI frameworks could erode Nvidia's pricing power faster than expected.
