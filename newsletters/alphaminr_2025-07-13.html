Digging Deeper Than the Headlines.

July 13, 2025

Has Jerome Powell lost his nerve in the face of Trump's trade war? President Donald Trump has pushed for the Federal Reserve to lower interest rates by "a full point" to juice the economy as inflation hovers near target, but Fed Chair Powell and his colleagues want to gauge the economic fallout from tariffs before making any moves. The stakes couldn't be higher as inflation was tame in May but economists expect Trump's tariff policy to raise consumer prices noticeably heading into summer .

The plot thickens as Trump announced new tariffs on 14 countries to go into effect August 1, including significant levies on Bangladesh, Cambodia, Japan, South Korea and others. The duties are similar to, and in some cases higher than, his April "reciprocal" tariffs, with Japan facing a 25% levy . Markets are processing the implications while Fed officials remain reluctant to move, fearful that tariffs could cause inflation in the coming months. "Everyone that I know is forecasting a meaningful increase in inflation in coming months from tariffs because someone has to pay for the tariffs," Powell stated .

Adding to the complexity, economists warn that "the tariff tax hike, restrictive monetary policy and worries about a further intensification of the trade war are weighing heavily on labor demand." Private payrolls excluding healthcare and education rose by just 23,000, well below the 50,000 average pace in the previous 12 months - a sign that seasonal adjustments may be masking underlying weakness .

Beyond the Headlines

The denim giant just showed inflation isn't dead yet. Levi Strauss (LEVI) surged 7% after crushing Q2 expectations with earnings of 22 cents per share on $1.45 billion revenue, demolishing estimates of 13 cents and $1.37 billion. The company flexed its pricing power by hiking its dividend and raising full-year guidance across multiple metrics . While other retailers struggle with discounting, Levi's premium positioning and brand power are letting it pass through costs.

Pentagon spending is back in vogue as defense stocks take flight. AeroVironment (AVAV) and Kratos Defense saw shares surge 9.5% and 10.7% respectively after Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth ordered the Pentagon to fast-track drone production . The move signals a major shift in military procurement strategy that could reshape the defense contractor landscape. With geopolitical tensions rising, drone makers are emerging as the new darlings of the defense sector.

The crypto winter gives way to a scorching summer. Coinbase (COIN) and MicroStrategy shares jumped 1% and 3% respectively as Bitcoin hit fresh all-time highs . The surge comes as Nvidia did something unprecedented - becoming the first company ever to reach a $4 trillion market cap after rising more than 2% . The convergence of crypto and AI continues to reshape the tech landscape.

Airlines finally catch a tailwind after a turbulent spring. Delta Air Lines (DAL) shares soared nearly 12% after reinstating its 2025 profit outlook that was pulled in April. While the new adjusted EPS guidance of $5.25-$6.25 is below January's $7.35 forecast, CEO Ed Bastian noted bookings have stabilized . The reset may finally give airlines the credibility boost needed to attract longer-term investors.

On the Horizon: Risks the Market is Missing

While investors cheer Amazon's (AMZN) Jeff Bezos offloading nearly 3 million shares worth $665.8 million in early July as part of his planned sale of up to 25 million shares through May 2026, the founder still holds over 900 million shares valued around $200 billion . The steady liquidation could create persistent selling pressure just as the company faces rising labor costs and antitrust scrutiny.

Tesla (TSLA) tumbled almost 8% after CEO Elon Musk announced plans to form a new "America Party." Investors have grown increasingly wary of the billionaire's political ambitions in 2025, with some analysts suggesting his forays into politics have damaged Tesla's brand and sales . With competition intensifying in the EV space, Tesla can ill afford self-inflicted wounds.

The mounting tariffs spell trouble for retailers, with analyst Mark Mahaney warning consumers will have to absorb higher costs. While giants like Walmart, Home Depot and Amazon can weather 10-30% cost increases, smaller retailers face an existential threat given retail's thin margins . A wave of bankruptcies could reshape the retail landscape by year-end.
